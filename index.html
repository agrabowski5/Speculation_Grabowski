Summary

Using spatial mapping, regression analysis, and integrated datasets, the research explores how corporate investments shape communities. It compares demographics in neighborhoods with high versus low corporate and absentee ownership, tracks changes in property flips over time, and examines the link between upscale new housing developments and corporate buying. These analyses reveal that speculative investments often target areas ripe for gentrification, demonstrate evolving investor behavior, and highlight an overlap between luxury developments and non-resident buyers. From the plot it is easy to discern that much more corporate investment is being made in white and wealthy neighborhoods, while whether it is through a flipping mechanism or just as a longer term hold investment. This is a trend that is consistent with the Boston Globe's analysis of luxury real estate purchases. This continuous development and speculation of luxury housing will continue displace low and middle income housing continueing to make Boston have unaffordable housing. The use of residential properties as investment vehicles rather than homes is a trend that is likely to continue make housing unaffordable for the average Bostonian.

Data Used, Limitations and Key Transformations

  • The primary data limitations arose from the varying geographic scopes of each dataset. For instance, the Corporate Ownership and Occupancy dataset did not cover all Boston neighborhoods, while the EDA dataset was even more geographically restricted. In addition, although the U.S. Census collects data every ten years, the census data spanning from 2004 to 2024 largely remained unchanged across my fields, which reduced the overall fidelity of my analysis. A key transformation applied to the data was merging tables by neighborhood instead of relying solely on Zip Codes, which was recently highlighted by NPR's Planet Money in their "ZIP Codes!" episode. Further transformations involved standardizing geographic classifications to align neighborhoods with logical regions on our Folium maps created in Python. Finally, regression analyses were performed to better understand the relationship between demographic factors and corporate ownership rates.
